Introduction

In 2015, Darmanis et al. published a seminal study exploring the cellular diversity of the human brain through single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq), marking a milestone in transcriptomics by providing an unprecedented resolution of cell-type-specific gene expression. The study, titled "A survey of human brain transcriptome diversity at the single cell level", utilized an early scRNA-seq pipeline to capture transcriptomic profiles from 466 single cells of the adult and fetal human brain.

Their findings offered insight into neuronal and non-neuronal transcriptional profiles and notably reported MHC Class I gene expression in adult neuronsâ€”a result that challenged existing assumptions.

Our Objective: Replicate and extend the analysis using modern tools (Scanpy) to validate findings and demonstrate reproducibility with contemporary pipelines.

Materials & Methods

Analysis Pipeline

1

Data Extraction

Combined raw .csv.gz files into unified gene count matrix using Python's pandas and gzip

2

Preprocessing

Quality control, normalization (sc.pp.normalize_total), log transformation (sc.pp.log1p), and HVG selection (top 2000 genes)

3

Dimensionality Reduction

PCA (50 components) followed by UMAP embedding (sc.tl.umap) for visualization

4

Clustering

Leiden algorithm (sc.tl.leiden) for community detection in k-nearest neighbor graph

Technical Implementation

The analysis was performed using Scanpy 1.9.0 in a Python 3.10 environment. Key parameters:

  • Normalization: Total-count normalization per cell
  • HVG Selection: Mean/dispersion method (sc.pp.highly_variable_genes)
  • PCA: 50 components using ARPACK solver
  • UMAP: min_dist=0.5, spread=1.0, n_neighbors=15
  • Leiden: Resolution=0.6

Key Findings

  • Reproduction of MHC-I expression in adult neuronal clusters
  • Improved resolution of glial subpopulations compared to original study

Discussion

Our reanalysis of the Darmanis et al. dataset using the modern Scanpy pipeline generally supports the conclusions of the original study while offering improved resolution and clarity. One of the most visually prominent results was the clear separation of neuronal and non-neuronal cells within the UMAP space.

Original Study (2015)

  • 8 major cell types identified
  • MHC-I expression in neurons
  • t-SNE for visualization
  • Hierarchical clustering

Our Reanalysis

  • 12 distinct clusters
  • Confirmed MHC-I findings
  • UMAP + Leiden clustering
  • Better glial subtype resolution

The use of Leiden clustering, compared to the original hierarchical methods, offered more granular identification of subclusters, potentially corresponding to finer distinctions between astrocyte, oligodendrocyte, and microglial subtypes. We observed some divergence in subcluster composition that may reflect improvements in normalization and variable gene selection offered by Scanpy.

Limitations

While our analysis benefits from modern tools, we recognize several constraints:

  • Limited metadata availability from original study
  • Potential batch effects not accounted for in original data
  • Comparison constrained by differences in clustering algorithms
